Fun with the Second-Person Plural

As a man who grew up in exurban parts of the American South, while some characteristics of my speech fit into an emerging, more homogenous dialect of 21st century American English, other parts of my speech are distinctly Southern.

My senior year of high school, I’d step out of my first period calculus class to do the school’s morning announcements. I’d get compliments for my “radio voice”, which I think was overstated but nonetheless implied a certain neutrality.

Moving to the Midwest after college, though, few would mistake me for a local, and, sure enough, on the excellent dialect map quiz from the New York Times, I scored a fairly accurate Greenville, South Carolina; Chattanooga, Tennessee; and Birmingham, Alabama.

The Word Y’all

It’s all well and good to be fixing to push the buggy over to the drink aisle to pick up some cokes for the party, sure, but my favorite feature of Southern American English is one of its best known: the second-person plural pronoun y’all.

It’s a smooth word, natural and generally friendly, and ever useful. Delightfully, it fills an uncomfortable void in more formal conjugation charts:

I am, you are, he/she/it is, we are, y’all are, they are

It has a comparably regional analogue in Spanish, at least, too:

Yo soy, t’u eres, e’l/ella/usted es; nosotros somos, vosotros seis, ellos/ellas/ustedes son

Recently, I’ve met some activist types who have adopted y’all as a gender-neutral replacement for you guys. To that I say, “Welcome aboard, y’all!” I’d like to elaborate two standards for usage that I think are commonly misunderstood.
